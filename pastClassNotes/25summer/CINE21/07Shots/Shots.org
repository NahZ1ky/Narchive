Notorious opens with a kind of quiet tension that just sits in your chest. from 0:01:25 to about 0:04:40, Hitchcock doesn’t waste a second: he’s already using the camera to tell you everything you need to know about Alicia before you even really meet her. the first shot is this wide, almost clinical look at the courthouse, all stone and shadow, people moving like they’re part of the building. it’s not just an establishing shot. it’s a reminder that the world is watching, and Alicia is on display.

as soon as we get inside, the camera starts to close in. there’s a medium shot of Alicia’s father being sentenced, but the real focus is on her. Hitchcock uses these tight closeups that almost feel invasive, like the camera is trying to get under her skin. you see her eyes flicker, her jaw tense. it’s not about the words, she barely says anything, it’s about the way she’s holding herself together. the implied proximity here is everything: you’re not just watching her, you’re with her, feeling the weight of the room pressing in.

then, as she leaves the courthouse, there’s this low angle shot that makes the building loom over her. Hitchcock used the environment to show how small and exposed she is. the light is harsh, almost cold, and it hits her in a way that makes her look both defiant and exhausted. it’s like she’s walking out of one prison and straight into another.

the next scene, at her apartment, is a total shift. the camera pulls back a bit, but the room feels crowded, almost claustrophobic. Hitchcock uses deep focus here, Alicia is sharp in the foreground, but you can see the party swirling behind her, all these blurred faces and bodies. it’s a visual way of saying: she’s surrounded, but completely alone. the lighting softens, but there’s still this haze, like she’s not really present. it’s beautiful and sad at the same time.

what gets me about these opening shots is how much Hitchcock is doing without calling attention to it. the angles, the focus, the way he moves the camera, it’s all invisible style, but it’s working overtime. you feel Alicia’s isolation, her vulnerability, and the way the world is closing in on her, all before the plot even really kicks in. it’s a masterclass in using cinematography to tell the story, not just show it.
