    After reading this article, I felt deeply shocked. A 13-year-old child, because of a moment of mistake, was sentenced to life imprisonment and spent nearly 20 years in solitary confinement. This is not just an unjust punishment but a distortion of the Florida Justice System. As an adult, I can understand the necessity of the justice system because society needs to control crime, but I can’t accept that such a young child is treated as an adult and sentence with such punishment so extreme that it almost meant to be torture. From this case, I see the coldness and irrationality of the U.S. justice system. Solitary confinement should only be a short-term measure for highly dangerous criminals, yet in this case, it became a daily torture for this young kid. This approach completely ignores the fact that minors are not fully developed mentally and directly robs them of the chance to reform. In such an environment, Ian was forced to rely on fantasies and desperate behaviors to survive—this was not the purpose of punishment but a twisted kind of suffering.
    As a logical person, I believe minors who commit crimes should of course, be punished. But that punishment should be educational, helping them recognize their mistakes and giving them a chance to rejoin society. Forcing a child to endure 18 years of solitary confinement only destroys their future and slowly erases their humanity. Sentencing a 15-year-old with punishment like this has almost no difference then robbing his life from him and sentencing him to death. I deeply sympathize with these young prisoners. They might have made mistakes, but those mistakes should not cost them their entire future.
