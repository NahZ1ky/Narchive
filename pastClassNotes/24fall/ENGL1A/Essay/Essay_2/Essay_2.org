    Social media's perpetuation of unrealistic beauty standards has caused significant harm to women's mental health and social well-being. "This creates a filter bubble ... often in the case of young women, the content we receive is of idealised faces and bodies—the Instagram face, the plastically firm Barbie body, If we ever look at a beauty post, or engage with beauty content, very soon we will be bombarded with posts which promote an unachievable beauty ideal." The National Eating Disorders Association reports a direct correlation between exposure to such images and a rise in body dysmorphia and eating disorders among young women (National Eating Disorders Association). Additionally, the constant exposure creates a cycle of comparison and self criticism, driving increased anxiety and depression. These impacts extend beyond individual mental health. The constant pressure to conform to these standards perpetuates systemic inequalities by reinforcing patriarchal norms. Women may feel compelled to focus on their appearance over other attributes, reducing their ability to challenge societal expectations. This issue highlights the need for both immediate action and long-term solutions to create a healthier, more comfortable environment for women.

    Social media companies must take responsibility by adjusting their algorithms to reduce harm and start promoting diversity, positive representations of beauty. Tech companies like Meta and ByteDance profit from algorithms designed to maximize user engagement, often at the cost of promoting harmful content. Studies indicate that these platforms amplify material showcasing unrealistic body types while downplaying diverse, unedited representations. For instance, Instagram's Explore page heavily favors highly curated, often altered images over natural ones (Rodgers). A solution could involve modifying algorithms to prioritize diversity in body types, ethnicities, and other natural appearances. Additionally, adding disclaimers on digitally altered images would provide transparency, helping users seperate reality from fiction. However, expecting tech companies to implement such changes without supervision is inpractical. Governments must intervene by enforcing laws that mandate transparency and accountability in content recommendation algorithms. For example, requiring platforms to submit to independent audits would ensure they address these harmful practices. Without these legal process being passed, it's unlikely for those tech giants to do anything intentionally harming their profit.

To amplify social media's impact, schools could pair these lessons with body-positive campaigns and partnerships with social media influencers who promote healthy and diverse ideals. The hard enforcement approach, drawing lessons from large-scale propaganda techniques like those used in China, can reshape societal attitudes toward beauty standards in a relatively short time. China’s state-led propaganda system effectively mobilizes mass media, education, and public discourse to instill values or beliefs across generations. By using repetitive messaging, controlled narratives, and strict regulation of public content, governments have historically shaped collective attitudes. For example, media bans and censorship of harmful imagery could force a cultural reset, promoting only diverse and positive representations of beauty. Such an approach could yield results in as little as a decade, but it comes with significant ethical concerns and probability of backfire. Hard enforcement may limit personal freedoms, and the process risks alienating individuals or groups who resist these changes. Additionally, unintended consequences, like over-correcting to an equally narrow set of ideals, could emerge. Despite the effectivity, this strategy requires careful balance to avoid creating a different kind of oppression.
    
    The softer approach, relying on gradual education and voluntary changes in societal norms, offers a more ethical but way slower path to addressing the issue. This method focuses on the general sense of societal communication, such as raising awareness and encouraging individual choices without heavy-handed regulations. Inspired by ideas from Claude Steele’s Whistling Vivaldi, promoting varied forms of communication encourages individuals to explore their identities in a positive way. Like widespread media literacy programs, partnerships with influencers who promote diverse standards, and campaigns showcasing unaltered beauty, slowly building up women’s trust in their own identities and help them understand that embracing their unique traits is nothing negative but a strength. Over time, these efforts can shift public perceptions. For instance, countries like Sweden have adopted body-positive campaigns with noticeable improvements in societal attitudes over decades. The challenge with this approach lies in its long timeline and the need for constant adaptation. Social media trends and technology evolve rapidly, so any progress can be undone if campaigns lose relevance or visibility. However, this method respects personal freedoms and allows society to organically adopt healthier values. Over time, with consistent effort, this approach can lead to a deeply rooted cultural shift without the backlash often associated with hard enforcement.
