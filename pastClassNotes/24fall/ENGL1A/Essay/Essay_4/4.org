    According to the CDC, for every eleven minutes, there is an American died because of suicide (“Suicide Data and Statistics”). One and a half years and two month ago, there were two point in time I almost contributed to that number. One and a half years ago, I was suffering from severe depression and multiple other mental illness, under harsh pressure from multiple sources about several matters. I called the suicide prevention hotline, "988", for myself before losing control of my own body and thoughts. No one picks up the call after a ten minutes wait. The ridiculousness of a hotline being such inresponsive made my laugh so hard and distracted me from the current stress I was in, the suicide prevention hotline ironically prevented me from committing suicide by not picking up the call. Despite 988's existence as a lifeline, the suicide prevention system in the United States often fails to intervene effectively due to systemic flaws, lack of accessible resources, and societal stigma, compounded by the absence of early prevention like parenting classes.

    The "988" suicide prevention hotline serves as the only and last hope for people on edge, but its effectiveness is lowered by incredibly long wait time and limited helps and resources provided to those in crisis. The call I made to the suicide prevention hotline last year was greeted by a cold, automated robot voice that ask me to wait for a crisis counselor to pick up the phone. I waited for around ten minutes and hung up out of impatience. Over the following year, I have called 988 severla more times. While I was patient enough to managed to connect to few conselor with the expectation of long waiting time, nearly all of them seems to follow the same general script, i have almost never felt warmth or empathy from their robotic tone. This experience isn't unique to me. many users from subreddit r/mentalhealth have shared similar frustrations, describing their experience with the crisis counselor was unhelpful, scripted, or even dismissive. u/AuntAoife describe their experience as such in one of their post: "It was like they handed me a band aid and patted me on the head when I was (metaphorically) bleeding out.", while there was support, no concret plan or help was ever delivered. u/Blacksunshinexo replied "15 minutes between texts and all they said was what I think I can do as a plan to help myself. [...] So worthless", further indicates that experience like this is not a outliner as we want it to be. There are countless posts with titles like "988 is the worst" and "In a crisis, 988 can't help." in this single community, and these are only the voices from those who survived. According to the concept of survival bias, the number of individuals who called 988 and found it almost useless is likely much higher with incountable others unable to share their experience because they ended their lives. In another way, they shared their experience encountering the hotline with action. While the hotline exists as a lifeline, its shortcomings sometimes turn it into a source of disillusionment for those it is meant to save.

    Hotlines like 988 are supposed to be a lifeline for people in crisis, but they are held back by two serious issues in my opinion, one of them, being the lack of funding. These hotlines are underfunded and almost impossible to keep up with the increasing number of calls they get. Because of this, people often end up waiting a long time just to talk to someone, and the resources available to help them are limited. The good new is that the Biden administration has aknowledged that and funded the department (“HHS Announces”) and the 988 program more than bilions of dollar over the course of two years (“Now in Its Second Year”). But those are one time fundings from the federal government, and the distributor, SAMHSA (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration) does not have control over the funding when it gets to the states level. By September 2023, most states has yet to propose a permanent funding plan for the project (Louis). Brian Stroh, CEO and medical director of Netcare Access gave a great annalogy on the funding problem of 988 used by KFF Health News, "What if we turned the tables a little bit and said we’re only going to fund 911 a little bit at a time? That’s a really hard proposition to work under" (ibid). These signs clearly shown that the white house recognizes the issues with the funding of the 988 project but not the project itself. Which causes the federal government does nothing but throws money at the state governments with uncertained frequency and amount, leaving the states themselves to handle the quality and supply of the crisis hotline. When in reality, funding the project more is nothing more than just the first step of improving it. With the recognition of the 988 project needs funding, the next step should be for both the federal and the state governments recognizing and fullfilling the need of passing bills and writing regulation on permanent funding of the project and the use of funding at the state level.

    The other issue which the hotline being held back is the quality of service provided by hotline and warmline operators, it is a critical problem yet does not get enough attention. A lot of people on edge, including me, have felt like the person on the other side of the line is just reading off a script. It’s frustrating and can makes you feel even more lonely. Countless survivers said they felt worse after calling because the operators seemed cold or dismissive in the subreddit we have discuss above. The training for the 988 operators and the protocols they follow need some big changes. There’s no nationwide regulation to ensure every operator is trained to the same level, or that they follow any effective protocols at all (DeGuzman). Instead, training and procedures vary wildly depending on the state, the funding, or even the organization managing the hotline (“FAQ: What Training”). This inconsistency indicates that two people calling for help could have completely different experiences, just because their number has different area code, or simply, called at different time. Some might get lucky with a skilled, empathetic operator, while some might stuck with someone who doesn’t know how to handle their crisis (DeGuzman), but lucky for them because they might just kill themselves right after instead of staying mad about the crisis counselor. Finally, without proper oversight, there’s no way to guarantee operators are doing their job well or improving over time. There are some local programs and orgnizations that have great systems with evaluations or retraining, but these are far from universal. This system at the nation level is full of gaps, leaving many people to question the reliability of these services in moments when there might be people's lifes hanging on threads, figurtively and literally.

    Improving hotlines like 988 starts with clear and uniformed protocal for training and rules for the operators. These hotlines are often the first point of contact for people in crisis, yet many operators are not fully equipped to handle the emotional intensity of these calls. They need more than basic training, as Dominick Flarey, the Executive Director of The American Institute of Health Care Professionals suggested, a crisis intervention specialist need to understand how to truly listen, stay calm, emotional resilianced, and offer real, meaningful support. Callers who reach out to these hotlines are often in their darkest moments, and a robotic response or a lack of empathy have total potential of makes the situation worse in every ways possible. Crisis counselors should be trained not just in crisis management but also in sounding caring and sincere, so callers feel like someone is genuinely on their side. Training should also cover how to guide people toward creating a plan for dealing with their crisis, giving them something solid to hold onto after the call ends. Regular updates and ongoing workshops would ensure operators stay sharp and continue to improve their skills. Another major issue is the inconsistency between states when it comes to the 988 hotline. Unlike 911, which has clear, nationwide protocols for handling calls, the 988 hotline is left largely up to individual states to manage. This leads to huge differences in the quality of care a caller might receive depending on where they live. While 911 operators are trained to follow strict, step by step guidelines, such as assessing the situation, prioritizing immediate safety, and dispatching appropriate resources (United States, National Emergency Number Association (NENA) PSAP Operations Committee and 9-1-1 Call Processing Working Group 8), 988 crisis counselors lacks this uniformity. Callers deserve the same level of consistency and professionalism when they are reaching out experiencing a mental health crisis. A set of national standards for how 988 calls should be handled could bridge this gap, ensuring that every person, no matter where they are get the same support. Mental health professionals must also play a bigger role in shaping both the training and the guidelines for 988. Similar to how 911 incorporates experts in emergency response, 988 could benefit from therapists, social workers, and crisis counselors helping to develop evidence-based methods for handling calls. Right now, hotlines often depend on generalists who follow scripts, but input from specialists could raise the service up to something truly life saving. These changes would not be quick nor easy, but they are absolutely necessary for the sake of countless vonerable lifes out there. The hotline has the potential to save those lives, but only if it invests in the people on the other end of the line and creates a system that is consistent, reliable, and always improving.

    The stigma surrounding mental health is one of the biggest reasons why so many people avoid reaching out for help, and it makes the suicide prevention system even harder to navigate. Mental health struggles are still seen by many as a sign of weakness, or something to be ashamed of, which discourages people from seeking support. For many individuals, there is a fear that reaching out for help will lead to being labeled as "weak", "crazy", or "unstable", and they believe that this label could follow them for the rest of their lives. This fear is a significant barrier to people calling hotlines, even when they’re in dire need of assistance. When someone is feeling hopeless, the last thing they need is to feel judged or misunderstood, but that’s often the reality. In fact, studies show that the fear of being stigmatized is one of the main reasons individuals don’t seek out mental health services (Henderson et al.). The stigma is often rooted in the societal belief that mental health issues are a personal failing, that they can be solved by “man up” or simply stop thinking negatively. This misunderstanding disregards the reality that mental health conditions are complex and influenced by a combination of biological, environmental, and emotional factors (“Mental Illness”). Overwriting the stigma means shifting the narrative, helping people understand that mental health is just as important as physical health and deserves the same care and attention. If the stigma surrounding mental health continues to thrive, people will continue to suffer in silence, leaving them without the support they need. Until mental health is viewed with the same empathy and understanding as physical ailments, individuals will remain unwilling to reach out to crisis hotlines or any form of suicide prevention, further undermining the system’s effectiveness in helping those in need.

    Social media has become a powerful tool for changing perceptions, and its unique position in modern human's lifes can help shift and reduce the stigma surrounding mental health issues. Platforms like Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube have massive counts of active audiences, especially among younger generations, making them an ideal space to start conversations about mental well-being. National campaigns that use relatable influencers and viral topics or hashtags can normalize mental health struggles and create a sense of community for those who feel isolated. For example, Canada's #BellLetsTalk campaign has succesfully generated widespread engagement and funding for mental health initiatives (Wikipedia Contributors). These types of campaigns encourages people to share their stories, fostering understanding and empathy across different audiences. The potential of social media goes beyond just awareness; it can directly guide individuals to resourses like crisis hotlines, therapists, or self-help tools. Unfortunately, many existing campaigns do not fully utilize these platforms to provide actionable steps for people who need immedeate assistance. Bridging this gap would involve collaborating with mental health organizations to create content that is both informitive and engaging, ensuring it reaches those most in need like how Harvard’s T.H. Chan School of Public Health teamed up with TikTok influencers (Muraskin) (“Partnering with”). At the same time, critics argues that social media also contributes to mental health problems, such as anxiety, depression, and self-esteems issues, due to cyberbullying, comparision culture, and overuse (Zhang). While these concerns are valid, utilizing the same platforms for positive campaigns could counteract some of these negative effects (Plackett et al.). Normalizing mental health conversations on these platforms could help reforming social media as a source of support rather than just a trigger for mental distress. In a world where milions of people scroll through social media daily, the opportunity to change the narrative around mental health is too significant to ignore. By using influencers, relatable messages, and accessible content, social media campaigns have the potential to break down stigma, educate the public, and ultimately, save lifes. If used effectively, social media could transform from a double-edged sword into a powerful tool in suicide prevention and mental health support.

    The stigma also often shown as an embeded fear in individuals about being diagnosed or hospitalized for their struggles. Society’s expectation of strength and independence pushes many to internalize their issues, avoiding professional help at all costs. A diagnosis is frequently seen as a lable that defines one’s character, leading to feelings of shame and insufficency. This fear is particularly evident in cases of severe mental illnesses like schizophrenia or bipolar disorder, where individuals are often illustrateded as dangerous or unstable in media and pop culture (Goepfert et al.). On top of that, the possibility of being hospitalized can be equally terrifying, this fear is worsen by the risks involved in reaching out for help. For example, 988 have drawn criticism for directly getting police involved or arranging involuntary hospitalizations when callers express thoughts of self-harm (Pattani). While these measures are intended to protect lives, they can backfired by discouraging people from asking help in the first place. Stories of individuals being handcuffed by police or confined in psychiatric wards after calling for support have gone viral on social media, fueling mistrust and fear toward these services (ibid). I have experience of being handcuffed by police as a 16-year-old personally, just because I shown thoughts of self-harm. Though, I only verbally express the thoughts but was calm in every other aspect and explained multiple times to the six police officers around that I am at a stable place mentally since I called for help myself, I was still being handcuffed for nearly an hour and involuntarily hospitalized for seven days. I was stable enough and been approve by the doctor on the second day but still held for five more days. That experience was trumatic, the days in the mental health institute made me more depressed because I wanted to be discharged so badly. By addressing the fear of diagnosis and hospitalization directly, society can create an environment where individuals feel safe acknowledging our struggles. This shift would not only comfort those in need to seek support earlier but also takes pressure off of an overburden mental health care system.

    The role of family in mental health is undoubtly one of the most significant yet overlooked aspects when examining the roots of emotional struggles. Toxic family environments, whether caused by abuse, absence, or excessive pressure, can play a major role in the development of mental health issues. For many children, home becomes a place of fear instead of safety, and those experiences tend to linger well into adulthood. Studies on Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) have consistently shown a strong link between childhood trauma and long term mental health problems (CDC). For example, children who grew up in homes with constant verbal or physical abuse are more likely to struggle with depression, anxiety, and self-esteem issues later in life. Parental neglect can be just as damaging as abuse. A lack of emotional support during the critical years of childhood forces children to developping complex feelings, often internalizing blame their own suffering. Exccesive pressure also contributes to mental strain. Parents who sat unrealistic standards on their children academically, personally, or socially can create environments where failure feels like an sin. Children raised under such pressure may develop long lasting stress and feelings of worthlessness, and these patterns of thinking will become increasingly harder to ease with age change (Kamis). For example, a kid being punished for every minor mistake will eventually avoid taking risks all together, out of fear of personal judgement and the possible consequances. The impact of toxic family dynamics does not end in childhood, it passes down the generations. Parents raised in harmful households are more likely to repeat these behaviors, whether intentionally or not, because it was the only parenting skill they learn growing up. Addressing these issues require early intervention and support system, such as family therapy or counseling, aiming at improving family relationships and promoting mental health education might break the cycle of trauma and provide healthier homes for future generations. Without proper intervention, toxic family environments will continue to be a silent contributor to the mental health crisis, leaving people to struggle with artificial scars that does not have to be there.
    
    Addressing the roots of mental health issues caused by toxic environments in families could be significantly helped by mandatory parenting education. The government need to pay for these parenting seminars through nearby colleges or universities and provide them to parents at no cost. aiming to provide parents with critical understanding regarding mental wellness, child psychology, and positive family dynamics. These programs could lessen instances of abuse, neglect, and emotional strain that frequently result in long-lasting psychological impairment by assisting parents in understanding how their parenting practices affect their kids' mental health. Parenting programs, for instance, can teach communication skills that assist parents in recognizing and understanding their children's emotions rather than disregarding them (Samfira), an issue that exists in many families. On top of this benefit, the classes could teach methods to handle stress and solve problems, ensuring that casual family disagreements do not turn into traumatic events. These classes would help both parents and children, creating a more supportive and caring home environment. In addition to this benefit, the classes might cover stress management and problem-solving techniques, keeping mild family arguments from slipping to traumatic incidents. Both parents and kids would benefit from these sessions, which would make the household a more loving and supportive place. The emphasis should be on preventing these problems before they arise, even if obligatory parenting programs are already in place in the United States for parents going through a divorce or those with a history of abuse (Shapiro et al.). Providing new parents with parenting education programs could contribute to the development of healthy family relations from the outset. Early access to these sessions can help parents provide secure, loving environments for their kids, lowering the possibility of emotional trauma and long-term mental health issues. These programs' accessibility is crucial. Parents of all economic levels, particularly those who might not be able to afford private parenting seminars, would be able to participate in free sessions with flexible schedules and online choices. It has been shown that including parenting education into early childhood or pregnancy preparation can help parents form positive habits before their kids are negatively impacted, which lowers the chance that they will experience mental health issues in the future (Costantini et al.). After all, introducing these classes as standard practice could be a significant step toward happier, healthier individuals, families, and society as a whole.

    That said, addressing the mental health crisis and preventing suicides requires urgent and thoughtful action from both the government and society. First and for most, the government must increase funding for suicide prevention hotlines and warmlines like 988, ensuring that regulations for training and day-to-day operations are in place to handle life or death situations. It is crucial that the government also utilizes social media wisely to counter stigma and make it easier for those in need to seek help. A step forward approach to cutting down mental health issues before they even begin is with  mandating accessible, government-operated parenting classes for new parents, to ensure that the next generation grows up in supportive and healthy environments. These challenges are not easy to overcome, but it is crucial for the government and society to take steps to implement these solutions or better alternatives. After all, the people hanging on the thread could be our very loved ones, and they all deserve to live a life on Earth where undoubtfully will be tedius without their presents.
